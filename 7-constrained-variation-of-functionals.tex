\section{Constrained Variation of Functionals}

To optimise a functional \(F\bqty{y}\) subject to a functional constraint \(P\bqty{y} = c\), some constant, we can introduce a Lagrange multiplier
\[
    \Phi_\lambda \bqty{y} = F\bqty{y} - \lambda \pqty{P\bqty{y} - c}.
\]

If we optimise with respect to \(y\), we have
\[
    \fdv{F}{y} - \lambda \fdv{P}{y} = 0,
\]
and optimising with respect to \(\lambda\), we have
\[
    P\bqty{y} = c.
\]

\begin{example}
    We determine the \(y\pqty{x}\) that minimises
    \[
        \int_{0}^{1} \frac{1}{2} \pqty{y'}^2 \dd{x}
    \]
    subject to the constraint
    \[
        \int_{0}^{1} y\pqty{x} = 1
    \]
    in the class of smooth functions on \(\ccintv{0}{1}\) with \(y\pqty{0} = y\pqty{1} = 0\). We have
    \begin{align*}
        \Phi_\lambda \bqty{y} &= \int_{0}^{1} \frac{1}{2} y'^2 \dd{x} - \lambda \pqty{\int_{0}^{1} y \dd{x} - 1} \\
        &= \int_{0}^{1} \pqty{\frac{1}{2} y'^2 - \lambda y} \dd{x} + \lambda.
    \end{align*}

    Let \(f = \frac{1}{2} y'^2 - \lambda y\). Euler--Lagrange Equation gives
    \[
        0 = \pdv{f}{y} - \dv{x}\pdv{f}{y'} = -\lambda - y'',
    \]
    and integrating twice gives
    \[
        y = - \frac{1}{2} \lambda x^2 + Ax + B
    \]
    for some constant \(A, B = 0\).

    Boundary conditions on \(y\) gives \(B = 0\) and \(A = \frac{1}{2} \lambda\). The constraint gives us
    \[
        1 = \int_{0}^{1} y \dd{x} = \bqty{- \frac{1}{6} \lambda x^3 + \frac{1}{2} \pqty{\frac{1}{2} \lambda} x^2}_{0}^{1} = \frac{\lambda}{!2}
    \]
    so \(\lambda = 12\), and \(y = 6x \pqty{1 - x}\).
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Dido's Problem]
    If we have a wall along the \(x\)-axis, and we build a fence around that wall, but we only have a fixed total length of fence \(L\), what shape should the fence to be to maximise the area enclosed? (See Example Sheet 1.)
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Isoperimetric Problem]
    A simple closed curve has length \(L\). What shape should it be to maximise its enclosed area? (See Example Sheet 2)
\end{example}

\subsection{Sturm--Liouville Problem}

Consider the class of functions
\[
    \contClassSymb = \set{\functiondomain{y}{\ccintv{\alpha}{\beta}}{\RR}}{y \text{ is }\contClass{2}\text{ and }y\pqty{\alpha} = y\pqty{\beta} = 0}.
\]

Let
\begin{subequations}
    \label{eq:sturm-liouville}%
    \begin{equation}
        F\bqty{y} = \int_{\alpha}^{\beta} \bqty{\rho y'^2 + \sigma y^2} \dd{x}
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}
        G\bqty{y} = \int_{\alpha}^{\beta} w y^2 \dd{X}
    \end{equation}
\end{subequations}
where \(\rho, w > 0\) on \(\oointv{\alpha}{\beta}\), and all of \(\rho, \sigma, w\) are functions on \(\ccintv{\alpha}{\beta}\). We want to minimise \(F\bqty{y}\) in \(\contClassSymb\) subject to constraint \(G\bqty{y} = 1\).

\begin{example}[Sturm--Liouville using Lagrange Multiplier]
    We optimise
    \[
        \Phi_{\lambda} \bqty{y} = F\bqty{y} - \lambda \pqty{G\bqty{y} - 1}.
    \]

    We verify that the boundary terms indeed vanishes in \(\var{\Phi_{\lambda}}\) vanishes.

    Therefore,
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:euler-lagrange-sturm-liouville}
        \fdv{F\bqty{y}}{y\pqty{x}} - \lambda \fdv{G\bqty{y}}{y\pqty{x}} = 0
    \end{equation}
    and we have
    \[
        \fdv{F\bqty{y}}{y\pqty{x}} = 2 \sigma y - \dv{x} \pqty{2 \rho y'} = 2 \mathcal{L} y
    \]
    where we define the operator \(\mathcal{L}\) to be
    \[
        \mathcal{L} = - \dv{x} \pqty{\rho \dv{x}} + \sigma.
    \]

    We also have
    \[
        \fdv{G\bqty{y}}{y\pqty{x}} = 2 w y.
    \]

    Hence, \autoref{eq:euler-lagrange-sturm-liouville} reduces to
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:eigenvalue-problem}%
        \mathcal{L} y = \lambda w y
    \end{equation}

    \autoref{eq:eigenvalue-problem} with the boundary conditions \(y\pqty{\alpha} = y\pqty{\beta} = 0\) is an \emph{eigenvalue problem}: only for discrete \(\lambda\) does there exist a non-trivial solution (eigenfunction) \(y\pqty{x}\). This will be studied in \textit{Methods} in more detail, and \(w\) is called a \emph{weight function}.

    Note this is a linear problem: if \(y \pqty{x}\) fixes this, then so does \(C y \pqty{x}\). Therefore, our constraint of \(G\bqty{y} = 1\) fixes a normalisation of \(y\). The putting \(y\) back into our functional, using integration by parts gives
    \begin{align*}
        F\bqty{y} &= \int_{\alpha}^{\beta} y \mathcal{L} y \dd{x} - \bqty{\rho y y'}_{\alpha}^{\beta} \\
        &= \int_{\alpha}^{\beta} \lambda w y^2 \dd{x} && \text{by \autoref{eq:eigenvalue-problem}} \\
        &= \lambda G\bqty{y} \\
        &= \lambda
    \end{align*}
    so the solution of our problem must be the lowest eigenvalue.
\end{example}

We may check that this also works if we change the boundary conditions to vanishing \(y'\) at endpoints, or vanishing \(y\) at one endpoint and \(y'\) at the other.

Recall that this is very similar to \autoref{ex:optimising-quadratic-form}. So we may do something similar to what we did back then -- converting this into a unconstrained problem..

\begin{example}[Sturm--Liouville using Unconstrained Optimisation]
    Define
    \[
        \Lambda \bqty{y} = \frac{F\bqty{y}}{G\bqty{y}},
    \]
    and we would like to optimise \(\Lambda\).

    We have (using chain rule)
    \begin{align*}
        0 &= \fdv{\Lambda}{y} \\
        &= \frac{1}{G} \pqty{\fdv{F}{y} - \frac{F}{G} \fdv{G}{y}} \\
        &= \frac{2}{G} \pqty{\mathcal{L} t - \Lambda w y}
    \end{align*}
    and the minimal value of \(\Lambda\) is indeed the lowest eigenvalue of the Sturm--Liouville problem.
\end{example}

\subsection{Geodesics on a Surface, Function Constraints}

Consider a surface \(S\) in Euclidean space, with some equation \(g\pqty{\vb{x}} = 0\). Let \(A, B \in S\), we want to find a shortest curve on \(S\) that connects these two points.

\begin{example}[Geodesics on a Surface]
    Let \(\vb{x} \pqty{t}\) for \(0 \l eq t \leq 1\) be a curve on \(S\), with
    \[
        \vb{x} \pqty{0} = \vb{x}_{A} \qand \vb{x} \pqty{1} = \vb{x}_{B}.
    \]

    We want to optimise the length \emph{functional}
    \[
        L\bqty{\vb{x}} = \int_{0}^{1} \sqrt{\dot{\vb{x}}^2} \dd{t}
    \]
    subject to a constraint given by a \emph{function}
    \[
        g\pqty{\vb{x}\pqty{t}} = 0.
    \]

    To do this, we use a Lagrange multiplier \emph{function} \(\lambda\pqty{t}\): let
    \[
        \Phi\bqty{\vb{x}, \lambda} = L \bqty{\vb{x}} - \int_{0}^{1} \lambda g\pqty{\vb{x} \pqty{t}} \dd{t}.
    \]

    Taking the functional derivative w.r.t. \(\lambda\) gives
    \[
        0 = \fdv{\Phi}{\lambda} = - g \pqty{\vb{x} \pqty{t}} \eqno{(\ast)}
    \]
    recovering the constraint; taking the functional derivative w.r.t. \(x_i \pqty{t}\) gives
    \[
        0 = \fdv{\Phi}{x_i \pqty{t}} = - \lambda \nabla_i g\pqty{\vb{x} \pqty{t}} - \dv{t} \pqty{\frac{\dot{x}_i}{\sqrt{\dot{\vb{x}}^2}}}. \eqno{(\dagger)}
    \]

    We want to eliminate the Lagrange multiplier function \(\lambda\). The trick is to differentiate \((\ast)\) w.r.t. \(t\), giving
    \[
        0 = \dot{x}_i \pdv{g}{x_i}
    \]
    and hence
    \[
        \frac{\dot{x}_i \pqty{\pdv*{g}{x_i}}}{\sqrt{\dot{\vb{x}}^2}} = 0.
    \]

    Taking another functional derivative give
    \[
        \dv{t} \pqty{\frac{\dot{x}_i}{\sqrt{\dot{\vb{x}}^2}}} \pdv{g}{x_i} + \frac{\dot{x}_i \dot{x}_j \pqty{\pdv*{g}{x_i}{x_j}}}{\sqrt{\dot{\vb{x}}^2}} = 0.
    \]

    Using \((\dagger)\), we have
    \[
        \lambda\pqty{t} = \frac{\dot{x}_i \dot{x}_j \pqty{\pdv*{g}{x_i}{x_j}}}{\sqrt{\dot{\vb{x}}^2} \pqty{\grad g}^2}
    \]

    Now, diving by \((\dagger)\) by \(\sqrt{\dot{\vb{x}}^2}\) gives
    \[
        \frac{1}{\sqrt{\dot{\vb{x}}}^2} \dv{t} \pqty{\frac{\dot{x}_i}{\sqrt{\dot{\vb{x}}}^2}} + \frac{\dot{x}_j \dot{x}_k \pqty{\pdv{g}{x_j}{x_k}}}{\pqty{\grad g}^2 \pqty{\dot{\vb{x}}}^2} \nabla_i g = 0.
    \]

    This is the equation for the geodesics on the surface, and we solve this along with \((\ast)\) which is the constraint that the curve lies on the surface.

    If we re-parameterise to use arc length as a parameter, we have
    \[
        \dv{l} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\dot{\vb{x}}^2}} \dv{t},
    \]
    and our equation now becomes
    \[
        \dv[2]{x_i}{l} + \frac{1}{\pqty{\grad g}^2} \dv{x_j}{l} \dv{x_k}{l} \pdv{g}{x_j}{x_k} \nabla_i g = 0.
    \]
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Geodesics for a Plane]
    We consider the constraint \(g = z\), i.e. the plane \(z = 0\). (The answer should be a straight line on this plane.)

    The second derivative
    \[
        \pdv{g}{x_j}{x_k} = 0
    \]
    and hence the geodesic equation gives
    \[
        \dv[2]{x_i}{l} = 0
    \]
    recovers a straight line indeed.
\end{example}
